Field notes from the free bin

Nobody Wants a Generic Hero. They Want SkyElite Private Jets.

I went through every free template on a popular motion-design site expecting landfill. Instead I found the cheat code, and it’s almost insultingly simple. The good ones aren’t good because of the animation. They’re good because they’re aggressively, deranged-ly specific. That’s the whole post. Stay for the jokes and five live demos.

June 20, 2026 · 8 min read SpecificityWeb DesignClaude

Here’s what I expected from the free tier of a template marketplace: beige. Lorem ipsum. A “Get Started” button hovering over a stock photo of two people pointing at a laptop and laughing about spreadsheets. The kind of filler whose entire job is to make you click “Upgrade.”

Here’s what I actually found. A hero section for a private jet charter called SkyElite. An NFT mint page for a company named Orbis. A data-security landing page for Securify. A booking flow for Datacore. A 404 page — a 404 page — with a complete fake brand identity called Nexto. And, I swear to you, an unsolicited styleguide for Duolingo, a company that did not ask.

None of these businesses exist. Someone sat down, invented a startup that sounds like it just closed a Series B, gave it a name made of expensive vowels, and then designed for that — instead of designing for “a SaaS.” And that, the whole choice, right there, is why the free one looks like it cost forty grand and your version looks like a hackathon.

The trick is they commit to a bit

Every free template that actually slapped did the same four things, and once you see it you can’t unsee it:

live demo · exhibit A · real template names, i did not make these up

One: a fake brand name that sounds VC-funded — Aetheris, Velorah, Neuralyn, Luminex. Pure vowel soup. Means nothing. Feels like it costs $90/month. Two: a specific vertical — not “business,” but private jets, biotech, data security for a company that booked you a meeting room. Three: a specific hero artifact — a dashboard, a 3D collectible, an audio player, an actual product doing an actual thing. Four: exactly one signature motion. Not ten. One.

Compare the forgettable ones. There’s a free hero literally called “Modern Agency.” Be honest — you forgot it while reading the words “Modern Agency.” Right next to it is “Logoisum Video Agency,” which is a fake company with a fake logo and a fake reel, and somehow that is the one that feels real. Specificity is not a garnish. It’s the meal.

Okay but why does specific win

Because your brain physically cannot picture “a SaaS.” Try it. Nothing. Static. But you can picture a black-and-gold private jet site with too much negative space and a font that thinks it’s an ad for a watch. A specific design does the imagining for the viewer, so it reads as a real product instead of a wireframe wearing a product costume.

It also makes every other decision for you, which is the actual luxury. The second you decide it’s “private jets,” the palette is decided, the type is decided, the motion is decided (slow, smug, gliding). “A modern website” has infinite correct answers, which is a polite way of saying zero. A constraint isn’t a cage. It’s the person who finally tells you what to order so you can stop reading the menu.

And there’s the cheap-but-effective part: a fake-yet-specific brand pattern-matches to real products you’ve already trusted, so your “this looks legit” reflex fires before your skepticism wakes up. It’s credibility cosplay. It works on you. It worked on me. I spent a Tuesday studying the marketing of jet companies that don’t exist.

The five tricks they wrap around the specificity

Here’s the part that should annoy you: the actual animation tech is free, public, and about as exotic as a sandwich. Every “how is this even built” effect below is running live on this page, written from scratch, no libraries. The specificity is the magic. These are just the five tricks it gets dressed in.

1. Scroll reveals (a.k.a. the entire personality of 90% of award sites)

Stuff fades up as it enters the screen. That’s it. That’s the effect that 90% of “cinematic” portfolios are built on. It is twelve lines of JavaScript and it has fooled the entire internet into thinking a budget was involved: 

const io = new IntersectionObserver((entries) => {
  for (const e of entries) {
    if (e.isIntersecting) { e.target.classList.add('in'); io.unobserve(e.target); }
  }
}, { threshold: 0.2 });
document.querySelectorAll('.reveal').forEach((el) => io.observe(el));

The one thing people botch is the easing. That cubic-bezier(0.22, 1, 0.36, 1) curve — fast start, slow landing — is the difference between “designed” and “PowerPoint.” Linear easing is the Comic Sans of motion. Every paragraph you’re reading floated in on that curve, and you didn’t even clap.

2. Word-by-word headlines (the “we have a film budget” flex)

Split the line into words. Stagger them. Take all the credit.

live demo · staggered text

Chop the headline into words, give each one a slightly later start, let the easing do the acting. The giant title at the top of this page is the same trick. Scroll it offscreen and back and it’ll perform again, like a needy little actor, because it is one.

3. Gradient backdrops (no, it’s not WebGL, please stop)

The big drifting gradient up top is four colors and one keyframe. That’s the entire spell. People see it and go “whoa, shaders” and I have to gently tell them it’s the CSS equivalent of moving a houseplant three inches every few seconds: 

@keyframes gradient-drift {
  0%   { background-position: 0% 50%; }
  50%  { background-position: 100% 50%; }
  100% { background-position: 0% 50%; }
}

Save the real WebGL for when you genuinely need fluid or noise that CSS can’t fake. Which is roughly never, on a landing page, for a jet company that isn’t real.

4. The cursor-tilt card (where the easing does 100% of the work)

Move your cursor over me

I track your pointer, turn it into a few degrees of rotation, and ease back to flat. Take the easing out and I become a haunted business card.

live demo · hover / move your cursor over the card

The geometry here is trivial — map cursor position to rotation, done. The entire “ooh, expensive” feeling comes from the eased transition that lets it settle instead of snapping. Rip that out and the card twitches one-to-one with your mouse like it owes someone money. The physics sells it. It’s always the physics.

So how does this help you (and your suffering AI)

Here’s the bit that loops back. The reason your AI-generated sites come out beige is the exact same reason the “Modern Agency” template is beige: you weren’t specific. “Make it pop” is the “Modern Agency” of prompts. When I want Claude to build something that doesn’t look like every other gradient-and-a-button site, I do this:

  • Invent the fake company first. “Build the hero for SkyElite, a private jet charter for people who sigh at commercial lounges” gets you a real design. “Build a SaaS hero” gets you sludge. Give the AI a thing to be about.
  • Name the effect, not the feeling. “Scroll reveal with ease-out-expo and a staggered word headline” beats “make it modern and clean,” which means nothing and everyone knows it.
  • Ask for one reusable piece at a time. A reveal. A marquee. Then snap them together. Small parts are easy to check; “build me a website” is how you get 1,400 lines you don’t trust.
  • Demand reduced-motion up front, because some of us get seasick. One prefers-reduced-motion media query turns all of this off. It’s not a bonus, it’s the rent you pay for using animation.
  • Write your own code. Don’t paste someone’s template and ship it — the techniques are generic, so the implementation may as well be yours. Everything here was built from the parts, not lifted from a gallery. (Yes, I’m aware of the irony of saying that in a post about studying other people’s templates. Studying the trick is allowed. Stealing the drawing is not.)

The actual takeaway, with the jokes turned off for one paragraph

The free template wasn’t more talented than you. It just had the nerve to be about something.

The libraries are free. The easing curves are public. The thing separating “looks like money” from “looks like a tutorial” is the willingness to commit to one specific, slightly absurd, fully-imagined idea instead of hedging toward “clean and modern.” Specificity is the budget.

So next time, before you write a line: invent the dumb specific brand. SkyElite. Orbis. Securify. Whatever. Design for that — the made-up company with the expensive vowels — and watch your generic side project quietly start to look like it raised a round. It was never the WebGL. It was that you refused to pick a lane.
