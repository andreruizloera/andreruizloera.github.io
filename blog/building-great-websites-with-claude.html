I did the homework so you don’t have to

How to Design Better with AI

I went through every free template on a popular motion-design site looking for sixty different ideas. I found one idea wearing sixty fake company names. The good ones aren’t good because of the animation — they’re good because each one is a disciplined, fully-committed lie. Here’s the autopsy, with evidence and five live demos.

June 20, 2026 · 9 min read Design AnalysisCoherenceClaude

Here’s what I expected from sixty-odd free templates: sixty different swings. Some good, most landfill, a couple of stock-photo handshakes welcoming me to Synergy. Range, basically.

Here’s what I got: the same playbook run sixty times, with the brand name swapped out like a hostage video. SkyElite Private Jets. Orbis NFT. Securify. Neuralyn. Datacore. Aetheris Voyage. A 404 page with a full corporate identity named Nexto. Once you’ve seen three, you’ve seen all sixty, and I’m about to ruin them for you forever.

The five moves. Every single one. No exceptions.

I read all of them so I could write this list, and then I read them again because I didn’t believe the list. Here it is. Every free template that actually looked expensive did all five:

  1. Invent a company that sounds funded. Not a real one. A believable one.
  2. Pick a vertical that earns the dark mode. SaaS, AI, jets, crypto. Never a dentist.
  3. Anchor on one concrete artifact. A dashboard, a 3D object, an audio player. A thing.
  4. Light it with exactly one ambient motion. One. Slow. The whole site, one mood light.
  5. Say one thing, with one button. One headline. One CTA. No menu of feelings.

That’s the entire trick, and it’s not “animation.” It’s that every single variable — the name, the color, the type, the artifact, the motion — agrees with the same imaginary company. Nothing contradicts. Nothing is filler. That total agreement is the thing your eye reads as “this cost money.” Let me prove each move, because I’m insufferable about evidence.

Move 1: the name is a believable lie

Look at the actual names. I’m not making these up — this is the marquee of real free template names, scrolling past you right now:

live demo · exhibit A · real names, zero real companies

Aetheris. Celestia. Luminex. Neuralyn. Nexora. Convix. There’s a recipe: take a Latin-ish or celestial root (aether, stella, lumen, orbis), bolt on a startup suffix (-yn, -ora, -ix, -core, -ify), and you’ve manufactured something that sounds like it has a Series B and a ping-pong table. It means nothing. That’s the point. A name that means nothing can’t contradict the design, so it’s free coherence. Meanwhile the forgettable free template is named, and I am quoting the site, “Modern Agency.” You already forgot it. So did I, mid-sentence.

Move 2: they only pick fights they can win

Catalog the verticals and a tell jumps out: it’s SaaS, AI, security, Web3, automation, private jets, a museum, a moody wellness brand. It is never a plumber. Never a dentist. Never “Greg’s HVAC, Family Owned Since 1998.” Why? Because those verticals don’t earn a black background and a slow purple gradient, and the template would look unhinged. The free templates quietly only ever pick subjects that justify the aesthetic they already wanted to use. It’s not range. It’s a casting director who only auditions people who already look the part.

Move 3 (the big one): one ambient motion, doing mood lighting

Here’s the move everyone gets wrong when they try to copy this stuff. Go look at the free animated backgrounds and they are all — every last one — the same temperature: Fluid Motion. Aurora Waves. Gradient Wave. Silk Ripple. Cosmic Ribbon. Slow. Drifting. Ambient. Not a single one is a fireworks show. The motion is lighting, not choreography. It sets a mood and then shuts up.

That restraint is the whole game, and it’s why your attempt looks cheap: you added ten animations because you could. They added one because they had taste. So — in the spirit of honesty — here are the actual effects, running live on this page, all written from scratch, no libraries. Every one is embarrassingly cheap. The discipline is the expensive part.

The drift you’ve been staring at. That gradient at the top is four colors and one keyframe. People see it and say “shaders?” and I have to tell them it’s the CSS equivalent of slowly sliding a houseplant across a windowsill: 

@keyframes gradient-drift {
  0%   { background-position: 0% 50%; }
  50%  { background-position: 100% 50%; }
  100% { background-position: 0% 50%; }
}

The scroll reveal. Stuff fades up as it enters the screen — the entire personality of 90% of “cinematic” portfolios. Twelve lines. It has fooled the whole internet into assuming a budget: 

const io = new IntersectionObserver((entries) => {
  for (const e of entries) {
    if (e.isIntersecting) { e.target.classList.add('in'); io.unobserve(e.target); }
  }
}, { threshold: 0.2 });
document.querySelectorAll('.reveal').forEach((el) => io.observe(el));

The only part people botch is the easing. That cubic-bezier(0.22, 1, 0.36, 1) — fast start, slow landing — is the difference between “designed” and “PowerPoint.” Linear easing is the Comic Sans of motion. Every paragraph here floated in on that curve and you didn’t even clap.

The “off” version isn’t broken. The words are fine, the layout’s fine. It’s just frightened — three buttons because it couldn’t commit to one, a headline that could belong to any company alive, a stock-photo-shaped hole where an idea should be. “On” isn’t more effort. It’s the same effort aimed at one specific lie and not blinking. 

Build ONE hero section for Orbis — a digital collectibles label that does
single timed drops. Five rules, no exceptions:

1. Brand: scarcity as luxury. It should feel like you are already late.
2. The vertical earns the look: collectibles, so commit to near-black, one
   electric accent, and a single hero object that looks worth owning.
3. One artifact: a 3D collectible, slowly rotating and bobbing, lit so the
   edges catch. No grids of thumbnails, no logo wall.
4. One motion: the object rotates; nothing else moves. Respect
   prefers-reduced-motion and let it sit still if asked.
5. One message, one button: a countdown-flavored headline and a single
   "Join the drop" CTA. No secondary links begging for attention.

Type: one confident display face, one clean sans. Numbers should feel minted.
Rule of thumb: if a choice doesn't make the drop feel scarcer, cut it.
Constraints over adjectives.

Read that prompt and notice what it mostly does: it removes options. One artifact, one motion, one CTA, “constraints over adjectives.” That’s the five moves rewritten as instructions. A great prompt isn’t a pile of adjectives — it’s a committed fiction with the exits welded shut.

Why “everything agrees” reads as money

Your brain can’t picture “a SaaS,” but it can picture a black-and-gold jet site with a font that thinks it’s a watch ad. A coherent fiction does the imagining for you, so it reads as a real product instead of a wireframe in a costume. And coherence is cheap to fake: once every element points at the same made-up company, your “this is legit” reflex fires before your skepticism wakes up. Expensive isn’t a budget. Expensive is the absence of contradictions.

Your AI prompts break all five rules

Here’s the loop-back. The reason your AI-built sites come out beige is the reason “Modern Agency” is beige: no committed fiction, no restraint. “Make it pop with lots of animations” violates the entire playbook in one sentence. When I want Claude to build something that doesn’t look like every other gradient-and-a-button site, I run the five moves at it:

  • Hand it the fake company first. “The hero for SkyElite, a private jet charter for people who sigh at commercial lounges” gets a real design. “A SaaS hero” gets sludge.
  • Name one motion, and ban the rest. “One slow gradient drift, nothing else moving” beats “make it dynamic,” which is how you end up with a carousel that autoplays into your skull.
  • Demand one artifact and one CTA. Give it a single thing to show and a single thing to ask. Coherence is just aggressively removing options.
  • Ask for reduced-motion up front, because some of us get seasick. One prefers-reduced-motion query turns all of it off. It’s the rent you pay for using animation, not a bonus.
  • Write the code yourself. Study the trick, not the drawing. Everything on this page was built from the parts, not lifted from anyone’s gallery — which, yes, is a slightly funny thing to say in a post that is a 2,000-word autopsy of other people’s templates.

The takeaway, jokes off for one paragraph

The free template wasn’t more talented than you. It just committed to one lie and refused to contradict it.

Sixty templates, one principle: pick a specific imaginary company, make every decision agree with it, light it with a single calm motion, and ask for one thing. Premium isn’t more — it’s coherence plus restraint around a committed idea. The libraries are free. The discipline is the product.

So before you write a line, invent the dumb specific company — SkyElite, Orbis, Securify, whatever — and then design for that one thing and nothing else. Pick a lane, light it once, say one sentence. Watch your generic side project quietly start to look like it raised a round. It was never the WebGL. It was that the good ones had the nerve to commit and the discipline to stop.
