Alex Karp and the Return of Belief

Reflections on Technology, Institutions, and the Future of the West

Alex Karp is not a normal CEO, and neither of these interviews feels like a normal book tour. In one interview, he argues that almost everything educated Americans have been taught about the world is intellectually incorrect. In the other, he jokes about spraying fentanyl-laced urine on financial analysts. At times, he sounds like a philosopher who accidentally became a billionaire. At other times, he sounds like a billionaire who discovered that nobody around him is willing to tell him to stop talking. Either way, he is difficult to ignore.

Both interviews center on The Technological Republic: Hard Power, Soft Belief, and the Future of the West, which Karp co-wrote with Nicholas Zamiska. The book argues that American technology companies have abandoned the ambitious projects that once made technological progress feel connected to national progress. Instead of concentrating on infrastructure, defense, manufacturing, and government, Silicon Valley has directed much of its talent toward advertising, entertainment, consumer convenience, and increasingly effective ways to keep people staring at screens.

Although the two interviews cover many of the same themes, they have noticeably different purposes. The CNBC interview is shorter and focuses primarily on the book’s political and institutional arguments. The longer conversation at the 92nd Street Y gives Karp more room to discuss Palantir, Germany, the Democratic Party, immigration, dyslexia, corporate values, and the unusual combination of philosophy and personal resentment that seems to motivate him.

I. CNBC Interview

“Palantir CEO Alex Karp: Everything You Learned in School About the World Is Intellectually Incorrect”
CNBC · Aired February 18, 2025

Karp begins the CNBC interview by joking that people normally write books for one of three reasons: to promote themselves, to promote their companies, or to prepare for a presidential campaign. He insists that his book serves none of those purposes. Instead, he presents it as an attempt to challenge the worldview that dominates American universities and other elite institutions.

His claim that “everything you learned in college is intellectually incorrect” is obviously exaggerated. Karp is not seriously arguing that calculus, biology, or every fact taught in college is wrong. He is attacking the political and intellectual assumptions that educated people are encouraged to accept without examining. In his view, universities teach students how they are supposed to interpret society but do not always encourage them to determine whether that interpretation produces good results in the real world.

Karp describes the book as a way of seeing the world that “may not be right, but it’s not wrong.” This initially sounds like a convenient way to avoid being held accountable for anything he says. However, his point seems to be that no political worldview perfectly explains reality. A useful worldview does not have to be correct about everything, but it should at least identify which ideas and institutions are obviously failing.

The United States, according to Karp, possesses an enormous advantage because it leads the global software revolution. That advantage will mean very little, however, unless American institutions can fulfill their basic responsibilities. Universities must educate people. Governments must function. Companies must create real economic value. The military must be capable of defending the country. An institution cannot survive forever by constantly failing at its stated purpose and then demanding that the public continue to trust it.

This leads to Karp’s argument that parts of Western society have developed what he calls a new “pagan religion.” He does not appear to mean paganism literally. He is describing an almost religious belief that anything benefiting America or demonstrating the effectiveness of Western institutions must be morally suspicious. Under this worldview, the West is automatically bad, and anything that works in the West becomes bad precisely because it works.

Karp calls this a religion because he believes its assumptions cannot be openly questioned. Someone who challenges the ideology is not merely treated as incorrect; that person is treated as immoral. The result is a strange environment in which people can criticize practically every part of American society except the intellectual framework through which that criticism is expressed.

Karp connects this ideological environment to the deterioration of public trust. When institutions repeatedly place politics above performance, people stop believing that anyone is truly an expert. My original note referred to “Ken Ero,” but Karp was discussing Kenneth Arrow, the influential economist and Nobel laureate. Karp’s example is that, during an earlier period of academic life, if someone as respected as Arrow said that a young scholar was brilliant, universities trusted his judgment. His recommendation carried weight because people believed both in his expertise and in the standards of the institution surrounding him.

Today, that kind of trust has largely disappeared. Karp does not think this happened simply because the public became stupid or irrational. He believes institutions damaged their own credibility by allowing political conformity, credentials, and internal status to become substitutes for achievement. His argument is not that experts should never be challenged. It is that institutions cannot function when nobody believes their standards represent anything real.

At one point, Karp describes the political climate as a pendulum. The cultural left swung strongly in one direction, and now a reaction is beginning to swing in the opposite direction. Intellectuals who once dominated the conversation are discovering that fewer people are listening to them. Instead of reconsidering their positions, some of them are becoming what Karp calls “protest parties”: groups that are increasingly angry and isolated but unable to build anything capable of gaining widespread support.

Karp connects this idea to his own childhood. He remembers attending protests in Washington, D.C., with his mother and realizing that nobody important was listening. He began his political life as someone protesting outside institutions. He eventually concluded that building something useful gives a person more influence than spending an entire life shouting from the outside.

That background makes Karp’s nationalism more interesting. He did not begin as someone who automatically trusted the government, the military, or major corporations. Nevertheless, he now argues that intellectuals and business leaders have a responsibility to remain dedicated to their country. A person can criticize the United States without acting as though its success is morally embarrassing.

His reference to Calvinism develops the same idea. Karp argues that American culture once assumed that building something significant indicated discipline, seriousness, and perhaps even moral character. Success did not automatically make someone a good person, but accomplishment was at least treated as meaningful evidence that the person had done something difficult.

Modern elite culture, in Karp’s view, has almost reversed this assumption. Success is frequently treated as evidence of exploitation, while failure can be reframed as proof of moral purity. Karp responds with a simple and slightly brutal question: What have you built?

He does not mean that everyone must create a multibillion-dollar technology company. A person could build a business, institution, intellectual theory, public program, road, weapon system, application, or any other project that functions outside the imagination of its creator. His standard is whether an ideology produces anything useful after it leaves the classroom.

This may be Karp’s most convincing argument. It is easy to develop a theory that sounds compassionate or sophisticated. It is much more difficult to construct something that actually works. At the same time, Karp sometimes acts as though building a successful company automatically proves that the builder’s entire political philosophy is correct. Palantir’s success may demonstrate that Karp knows how to build a valuable software company. It does not necessarily make him correct about every question involving education, politics, immigration, or the future of Western civilization.

II. The 92nd Street Y Interview

“Palantir CEO Alexander Karp with Andrew Ross Sorkin: The Technological Republic”
Interviewer: Andrew Ross Sorkin · The 92nd Street Y, New York · Recorded February 18, 2025

The first thing I noticed about the 92nd Street Y interview was how Karp presented himself. He sat slouched comfortably in his chair, wearing jeans, bright blue socks, and matching blue shoes. He did not look like someone carefully managing the public image of a major corporation. He looked like someone sitting in his living room who had temporarily forgotten that an audience was watching.

This comfort extends to what he says. Early in the conversation, Karp explains his idea of a “lower purpose” by joking about using a drone to spray fentanyl-laced urine on financial analysts who attempted to damage Palantir. It is a ridiculous statement and probably one that most public-company CEOs would have possessed the basic judgment to keep inside their heads. However, it also reveals something important about Karp. His public personality combines serious philosophical beliefs with pettiness, resentment, and a nearly complete refusal to speak in the sanitized language normally expected from corporate executives.

Andrew Ross Sorkin asks Karp about a passage in the book arguing that young people have been robbed of the opportunity to develop their own view of the world. Karp believes that many young people were never encouraged to create a genuine belief structure through experience. Instead, they were handed a collection of approved political positions and taught to repeat them.

This connects to his criticism of companies that constantly broadcast their political opinions. During the past several years, corporations began releasing statements on practically every major political and social controversy, including controversies that had little connection to their actual businesses. Karp sees much of this behavior as performance rather than principle.

He is not arguing that technology companies should possess no values. Palantir clearly has strong values, particularly regarding the United States, its allies, Israel, and the American military. The distinction is between genuinely possessing values and publicly repeating fashionable positions. A company’s real beliefs are not revealed by the slogans it posts online. They are revealed through the customers it accepts, the work it refuses, and the financial or political risks it is willing to take.

Karp believes the strongest leaders possess the strongest internal belief structures. Leaders without genuine convictions are forced to determine what they are currently supposed to pretend to believe. This is how he interprets the failures of several university presidents. In his view, they did not necessarily lose their jobs because they believed the wrong thing. They lost their jobs because, once placed under pressure, they could not determine which position they were expected to perform.

Karp’s use of the phrase “pagan religion” becomes clearer here. People are expected to profess an approved set of beliefs, but the beliefs are often disconnected from their actual conduct. A university, corporation, or political party can describe itself as compassionate, inclusive, and democratic while behaving in ways that contradict those values. What matters is not whether the belief is true but whether the person knows the correct words to say.

Sorkin examines this issue through the ideas of Milton Friedman, who famously argued that a corporation’s primary responsibility is to increase profits for its shareholders while obeying the law. The conversation is interesting because Palantir is both intensely ideological and extraordinarily valuable. Karp argues that many of Palantir’s most financially valuable decisions were originally made because of the company’s principles, not because they appeared to offer the greatest immediate profit.

Palantir’s values occasionally pushed it toward decisions that seemed commercially irrational in the short term. Over time, those decisions strengthened the company’s identity and created long-term value. Karp is therefore not completely rejecting Friedman. He is arguing that a company with genuine convictions and a sufficiently long time horizon may create more shareholder value than a company constantly chasing whatever appears profitable during the next quarter.

His claim that Palantir sued the United States government “as an act of love” sounds contradictory, but it fits this philosophy. Palantir sued the U.S. Army because it believed the Army had improperly ignored commercially available software while attempting to develop its own system. Karp views the lawsuit not as an attack on the country but as an attempt to make an institution he valued work better.

For Karp, loyalty does not require passive obedience. Someone who genuinely cares about an institution should be willing to criticize it when it fails. The purpose of the criticism, however, should be to improve the institution rather than to prove that the institution never deserved to exist.

This attitude also explains his admiration for the American military. Karp describes it as one of the strongest and most meritocratic institutions in the country because accomplishment continues to matter. A person may dislike a particular president, war, policy, or military operation. Karp argues that it does not logically follow that the United States government or military is inherently illegitimate.

His argument becomes more controversial when he turns toward the Democratic Party. Karp has historically identified with Democrats, but he believes the party has become extremely difficult to criticize from within. People raise concerns about unpopular or unsuccessful policies, nobody listens, and then party leaders act surprised when voters reject them.

Karp’s message to Democrats is essentially to stand up and admit when something does not work. A policy can originate from a compassionate belief and still produce terrible results. It is not enough to say that an ideology is morally correct. A political ideology also needs to be capable of winning elections, governing institutions, and surviving contact with reality.

That is what Karp means when he says it is important to possess an ideology that can win. Someone eventually needs to say, “You can believe in this, but it will not work.” He is not necessarily asking people to abandon their principles. He is asking them to recognize that good intentions do not automatically create good outcomes.

Karp repeatedly returns to Germany as an example. The German Green Party, formally known as Alliance 90/The Greens, is an environmental and center-left political party that has generally supported liberal immigration policies, European cooperation, and programs intended to help migrants integrate into German society.

My note that the Greens controlled twenty percent of Germany was not quite right. The party was influential because it participated in Germany’s governing coalition and controlled important ministries. In Germany’s February 2025 federal election, however, the Greens received 11.6 percent of the party vote and won 85 seats in the Bundestag.

Karp continues returning to immigration because he sees it as one of the clearest conflicts between moral aspiration and practical sustainability. A political party can support welcoming immigration policies for sincere humanitarian reasons, but it must still confront questions involving borders, housing, integration, wages, crime, public services, and democratic consent.

A government cannot simply accuse voters of immorality when they believe a policy is failing. Whether or not Karp’s own position on immigration is correct, his larger argument is that political leaders must be able to acknowledge practical consequences. An ideology that cannot admit failure eventually loses its ability to govern.

Karp applies the same standard to international institutions such as the United Nations. He does not appear to argue that these institutions should disappear. He argues that they need to become better at fulfilling their stated purposes. An institution cannot defend its legitimacy entirely through its historical reputation. It must still accomplish something.

Another of Karp’s memorable statements concerns how software companies treat their customers. In one of his cruder descriptions, he suggests that companies often pretend to satisfy the client while actually exploiting the client. The phrasing is vulgar, but the meaning is straightforward. Many technology businesses present themselves as serving users while designing products that make those users dependent, distracted, or easier to monetize.

This connects to his statement that when people sell you pleasure, you are probably their product. Social media, entertainment platforms, and consumer applications may appear to sell enjoyment or convenience. In reality, they often sell the user’s attention, behavior, or personal information to someone else.

Karp’s criticism is that Silicon Valley’s smartest people have spent too much time perfecting systems of consumption. The technology may be sophisticated, but its final purpose is often remarkably unambitious: make people click more advertisements, watch more videos, buy more products, or remain on an application for a few additional minutes.

The main thesis of The Technological Republic is therefore not simply that companies should become more political. It is about how to construct a company capable of producing transformative technology. Karp wants technology companies to work on problems involving defense, manufacturing, medicine, infrastructure, and government instead of limiting themselves to consumer pleasure.

At one point, Karp says that the political pendulum may never swing back, especially in technology. In the CNBC interview, his pendulum metaphor sounds like a normal political reaction: one side moves too far, and eventually the public pushes back. In the 92NY conversation, he introduces a more unsettling possibility. Artificial intelligence and software may transform society so quickly that there will be no return to the old equilibrium.

Countries that fail to build new technologies may not get another opportunity once the political climate changes. By the time they reconsider their choices, the companies, talent, capital, and technical knowledge may already be concentrated somewhere else. The pendulum cannot swing back if the structure holding it has been replaced.

Karp illustrates this gap with Palantir’s growth. In my notes, “comm” referred to commercial revenue. Palantir reported that its U.S. commercial revenue grew 54 percent during 2024. Karp contrasted the company’s rapid American growth with much weaker performance in continental Europe. The comparison is not a complete measure of the European economy, but his larger warning is clear: Europe risks becoming a place that regulates and debates technologies built in the United States rather than a place that builds those technologies itself.

Karp does not believe American technological success will remain politically sustainable unless its benefits reach ordinary people. The United States cannot become a country in which the wealthiest one percent becomes ten times richer while the average person experiences little improvement. If artificial intelligence creates enormous wealth for a small number of founders, engineers, and investors while replacing everyone else’s jobs, the public will eventually reject the entire technological project.

This is what Karp means when he asks how more people can participate in American GDP growth. Technological progress cannot feel like a private miracle for a tiny group and a public disaster for everybody else. For technology to maintain democratic legitimacy, ordinary people need to experience some improvement in their wages, opportunities, security, or quality of life.

Sorkin contributes an interesting geopolitical example by describing a conversation with a Chinese executive. According to Sorkin, the executive said that China could tolerate the United States leading the technology race but did not want America to completely win it, because winning would create a zero-sum outcome.

That distinction captures what Karp believes is at stake. Artificial intelligence is not merely another profitable industry. The country that dominates it may gain military advantages, economic leverage, intelligence capabilities, and the ability to establish the technical standards followed by the rest of the world. From Karp’s perspective, refusing to compete does not create a peaceful, neutral world. It merely allows someone else to win.

For someone so closely associated with technological innovation, Karp is surprisingly suspicious of novelty. He argues that people naturally assume the newest idea or product must be better than whatever existed before it. His advice is almost the opposite: when something genuinely works, hold on to it for dear life.

Innovation should not mean destroying a functioning institution simply because it is old. The goal should be to preserve what remains valuable while replacing what clearly fails. This is a more conservative argument than people might expect from someone running an artificial-intelligence company.

Karp’s personal advice follows the same philosophy. He recommends putting one’s entire body and mind into something meaningful for approximately five years. Instead of constantly switching interests or optimizing every minor decision, a person should select a difficult project and remain committed long enough to discover whether it can become important.

He believes people need both a higher purpose and a lower purpose. The higher purpose gives the work meaning beyond money or personal status. The lower purpose may involve competition, anger, pride, or the simple desire to defeat someone who doubted you. Karp’s own higher purpose is strengthening the United States and its allies. His lower purpose apparently involves fentanyl-laced urine and revenge against analysts.

Karp also discusses his relationship with Palantir co-founder Peter Thiel. The two reportedly argue about politics and philosophy, but Karp considers those arguments essential to their work rather than distractions from it. Political philosophy affects which products Palantir builds, which governments it supports, which customers it rejects, and which risks it considers acceptable.

At Palantir, according to Karp, accomplishment is supposed to matter more than fitting into a recognizable professional category. Credentials and appearance may help someone enter an institution, but they should not matter more than what the person can actually do.

The final discussion about dyslexia initially confused me, especially when Karp suggested that everyone should learn to be dyslexic. He obviously does not mean that people should attempt to acquire a learning disability. He is describing the habits that dyslexia forced him to develop.

Because some conventional academic tasks were difficult for him, Karp had to determine quickly what he was bad at and identify the areas in which he possessed an unusual advantage. He could not simply follow the standard path and expect the standard system to reward him. He had to approach information differently.

Karp believes dyslexia also created distance between him and what he calls “taxonomy,” meaning the official categories through which people are taught to understand the world. Someone who processes information conventionally may absorb those categories so naturally that they become difficult to question. Because Karp struggled with the conventional process, he learned to see the categories as human creations rather than permanent truths.

“Learning to be dyslexic” therefore means learning to think without allowing existing categories to control every conclusion. It means refusing to confuse credentials with intelligence, persuasion with truth, or conformity with accomplishment. A person should find the one or two areas in which they may be genuinely exceptional and accept that being average at many other things is not a tragedy.

Conclusion

The two interviews present different versions of the same Alex Karp. In the CNBC appearance, he delivers a compact argument about national loyalty, institutional failure, elite ideology, and the importance of building things. At the 92nd Street Y, he expands that argument into a broader and much stranger philosophy involving Palantir, corporate values, Germany, immigration, wealth inequality, dyslexia, Peter Thiel, and urine-based drone warfare.

I do not find every Karp argument convincing. His tendency to divide the world into courageous builders and useless intellectuals can become exhausting. He occasionally treats Palantir’s financial success as though it proves his entire political philosophy. His jokes can distract from his serious ideas, and his confidence regularly crosses into self-satisfaction.

Nevertheless, he asks questions that most corporate leaders carefully avoid. What do our institutions actually accomplish? What beliefs are we willing to defend when they become unpopular? Does technology make society stronger, or does it merely make a few companies richer? Are our beliefs genuinely our own, or are we repeating what our institutions expect us to say?

Most importantly, Karp asks what we have actually built. That question connects both interviews. Belief without action becomes performance. Criticism without construction becomes protest for its own sake. Technology without purpose becomes another system for collecting attention and selling advertisements.

Even when Karp is excessive, vulgar, or possibly wrong, he forces the listener to consider whether Western institutions still possess the confidence to create anything ambitious—and whether the people inside those institutions possess convictions that are actually their own.
