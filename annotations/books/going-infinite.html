Going Infinite

Michael Lewis · 2023 · A chapter-by-chapter review (DNF)

Overview

Michael Lewis’s Going Infinite is supposed to explain the rise and fall of Sam Bankman-Fried, the founder of FTX. At least in the first half of the book, however, it often feels less like an investigation and more like the construction of a legend. Lewis is clearly fascinated by Sam. He presents him as brilliant, socially strange, indifferent to wealth, and almost completely free from ordinary human desires. Everyone around him wants money, status, fame, or access, while Sam supposedly wants only to make as much money as possible so that he can improve the world.

That framing is interesting at first, but it quickly becomes difficult to trust. Sam is obviously intelligent and unusual, but Lewis often seems so impressed by him that he fails to question whether the stories he tells are actually flattering. The result is a book that is often entertaining but also strangely defensive of its subject.

Preface

The book begins with Michael Lewis meeting Sam because one of Lewis’s friends is considering exchanging hundreds of millions of dollars’ worth of shares with him. The friend understands FTX reasonably well but cannot figure out Sam himself, so he asks Lewis to meet him and report back.

Lewis immediately focuses on Sam’s appearance. He arrives wearing a T-shirt, cargo shorts, old sneakers, and limp socks, looking more like someone about to wander through a forest than one of the richest people in the world. This becomes central to the image Lewis creates. Sam does not dress like a billionaire, act like a billionaire, or appear interested in the things billionaires normally care about.

Instead, he claims that he wants “infinity dollars” so that he can address enormous problems such as pandemics, nuclear war, dangerous artificial intelligence, and threats to democracy. He is not supposed to want wealth for himself. He wants wealth only because wealth can be converted into good.

Lewis comes away from the meeting completely sold on him and advises his friend to make the deal. Only afterward does Lewis realize that he never answered the original question: Who exactly is Sam Bankman-Fried?

That question is supposed to drive the rest of the book. My problem was that Lewis already seemed to have decided on the answer. Sam was going to be the strange, misunderstood genius who did not care about money and only wanted to save humanity.

I was skeptical almost immediately.

Chapter 1: “Yup”

The first chapter introduces Natalie Tien, who joins FTX with little public-relations experience and quickly becomes one of the main people managing Sam’s life. She schedules his interviews, organizes his flights, deals with journalists, and generally tries to create order around a person who seems to have no interest in creating any for himself.

The chapter also shows how quickly Sam became famous. Suddenly, everyone wanted to meet him. He was spending time with Shaquille O’Neal, the Kardashians, politicians, journalists, and major business figures.

I found this part both pathetic and deeply sad. These people had almost nothing in common with Sam, yet they desperately wanted to be around him because he was worth billions of dollars. In what universe would Sam Bankman-Fried, Shaq, and the Kardashians naturally become friends? The obvious answer is the universe in which one of them has enough money to become useful to everyone else.

There is a connection here to René Girard’s theory of mimetic desire. People often decide that something is valuable because they see other people desiring it. Once a few powerful people wanted access to Sam, everyone else wanted access to him too. His importance became self-reinforcing. People wanted to meet him because important people wanted to meet him.

The title of the chapter comes from the way Sam responded while people spoke to him. He would often be playing a video game or working on something else, occasionally saying “yup” to create the impression that he was listening. According to Natalie, the longer he stretched out the word, the less likely it was that he had heard anything.

The funniest example is his Zoom call with Anna Wintour. While she speaks, Sam minimizes her window and starts playing Storybook Brawl. Somehow, by the end of the conversation, she believes that he might attend the Met Gala and possibly help fund it.

I was surprised by how shamelessly opportunistic Anna Wintour seemed. She did not come across as someone who cared about Sam as a person. She seemed interested in what his money could provide. I have met plenty of people like that—people who appear only when they think they can receive something from you. They act friendly, but the friendship disappears the moment you are no longer useful. It is pathetic.

Natalie nevertheless describes Sam as a genuinely nice person. She says that he was never cruel or inappropriate with her and that, unlike many other men in crypto, he did not seem driven by money, sex, or status.

That becomes the central image of Sam in the chapter. He is supposedly free from ordinary human temptations. He does not care about wealth, women, fashion, celebrities, fame, or social approval. He is apparently just a harmless genius trying to save the world while playing video games during every conversation.

The chapter is entertaining, but it also introduces one of my main problems with the book. Lewis seems determined to portray Sam as an innocent outsider surrounded by vultures. Everyone else is chasing money and access, while Sam somehow floats above all of it.

Chapter 2: “The Santa Claus Problem”

The second chapter goes back to Sam’s childhood. He was clearly not a normal child. His mother would take him to amusement parks, but he did not enjoy them in the way other children did. He was constantly bored and seemed much more interested in mathematics, puzzles, games, and conversations with adults.

He also had trouble understanding how anyone could genuinely believe in Santa Claus or God. Both ideas seemed so obviously invented to him that he did not initially consider the possibility that other people sincerely believed them. This led him to a broader realization: huge numbers of people could all believe something that was completely wrong.

His relationship with his younger brother, Gabe, was also distant. Gabe describes living with Sam as something closer to sharing a house with another tenant than growing up with a close brother. That detail stood out because it suggests that Sam’s emotional distance was not created by wealth or fame. It had been present almost his entire life.

His parents, Joe Bankman and Barbara Fried, were both professors at Stanford Law School. They are portrayed as extremely intelligent, deeply liberal, and largely uninterested in ordinary conventions. They did not marry and instead entered a domestic partnership partly in solidarity with gay friends who were legally unable to marry at the time.

Sam himself says that he remembers very little of his childhood. What is stranger is that he does not seem particularly interested in remembering it. Lewis searches his childhood for clues about the person he became, while Sam seems to regard his own childhood as mostly irrelevant information.

What he does remember is being bored.

He was very good at mathematics and eventually attended Canada/USA Mathcamp. There, he finally met other people who were unusually interested in mathematics, games, and puzzles.

His childhood friend Matt Nass later helped create Storybook Brawl, which became Sam’s favorite video game. That is honestly an absurd coincidence. Out of all the games Sam could have become obsessed with, his favorite was eventually created by someone he had known since childhood.

Sam also loved games involving incomplete information and uncertain outcomes. This was why he enjoyed Magic: The Gathering. He preferred games in which skill mattered but did not completely determine the result. He was less interested in games like chess, where both players could see the entire board and where there was theoretically a correct move in every position.

Matt describes Sam as a very nice person, and Lewis continues constructing the same image from the first chapter. Sam is brilliant, emotionally detached, bored by almost everything, and fundamentally harmless.

At the same time, I think the book understates how much Sam clearly enjoyed attention. He created public puzzle hunts and posted his philosophical ideas online. That does not automatically make him a narcissist, but it complicates the claim that he did not care what people thought of him.

Maybe he did not want ordinary approval. He did not care whether people liked his clothes or thought he was socially impressive. But he clearly wanted people to recognize that he was unusually intelligent.

Lewis also describes Sam practicing facial expressions because normal emotional reactions did not come naturally to him. He learned what his face was supposed to look like in different situations and trained himself to produce the appropriate response.

That is fascinating, but also fairly creepy.

By the end of the chapter, Sam has been presented as a super-genius who is nice, bored, emotionally distant, and naturally attracted to probability and uncertainty. It is an interesting portrait, but Lewis is already laying the genius narrative on extremely thick.

Chapter 3: “Meta Games”

I honestly disliked this chapter.

It focuses mainly on Sam’s time at MIT and his attempt to figure out what he wanted to do with his life. He considered becoming an academic but eventually decided that academia was too slow and boring for him.

Around this time, he was introduced to effective altruism, particularly through Will MacAskill and other effective altruists connected to Oxford. Their argument was that a talented person might do more good by entering a highly paid profession and donating the money than by working directly for a charity.

This idea is known as “earning to give.” Instead of becoming a doctor and treating patients personally, for example, someone could become a trader, earn millions of dollars, and use that money to fund several doctors.

The basic argument is interesting. My problem is that the book presents effective altruism with very little serious criticism. There is barely any discussion of competing ethical theories or of the danger involved in reducing every moral decision to a calculation.

Sam then becomes interested in quantitative trading and joins Jane Street. The firm’s interviews and internships involve betting games, probability questions, changing rules, and situations where participants must make decisions with incomplete information. Sam is naturally good at this because it resembles the games and puzzles he already enjoys.

The part that really bothered me was the story involving Sam and another intern, Asher Mellman.

Sam and Asher created a contract based on the greatest amount of money any intern would lose through betting that day. Sam bought the contract and then began encouraging other interns to make large coin-flip bets against him. By creating a large loss for either himself or someone else, he could increase the value of the contract he had purchased.

Lewis presents this as a brilliant trap. Sam supposedly sees something Asher does not and completely outsmarts him.

The problem is that Lewis’s explanation is mathematically shaky. If Sam himself loses the coin flip, the money he receives from the contract reduces his total loss, which then affects the amount the contract is supposed to pay. The contract becomes partly self-referential.

Even worse, after Sam had already completed the clever part of the trade, he apparently continued gambling. Those additional bets did not necessarily improve his position. He seems to have kept going partly because he enjoyed winning and humiliating Asher.

This is where the constant language of expected value and “theo” began to annoy me.

The first part of Sam’s trade may have been clever, but the story is not the clean mathematical “gotcha” Lewis wants it to be. Once Sam continued betting, it started to look less like pure expected-value maximization and more like someone who enjoyed gambling and proving that he was smarter than another person.

Sam even seems to understand that he is being cruel to Asher. He simply decides that proving his point matters more than Asher’s feelings.

That is not the same as being socially unaware. It means understanding that another person is upset and deciding not to care.

This chapter left such a sour taste in my mouth that I considered not finishing the book. I continued because I was already halfway through and because I had spent too much money on it.

More broadly, this is where I started to feel as though Michael Lewis was falling in love with his subject. Sam is constantly presented as more intelligent, creative, unconventional, and rational than everyone around him.

What surprised me was that Lewis treated Sam’s personality as though it were almost unprecedented. I have met people extremely deep in mathematics, including Ph.D. students at top universities, USAMO qualifiers and winners, and IMO gold medalists. Many of them are highly unusual people who genuinely do not care about ordinary social conventions.

That does not mean Sam was not intelligent. He obviously was. But being brilliant, socially strange, and indifferent to convention is not nearly as rare in high-level mathematics as Lewis seems to believe.

Chapter 4: “The March of Progress”

This chapter takes the reader onto the Jane Street trading floor and gives a basic explanation of how the firm makes money. I found this section fairly interesting, especially the story about the 2016 presidential election.

Sam helped lead a project to collect election results directly from individual states before television networks reported them. The system worked. Jane Street was sometimes receiving information minutes, and occasionally much longer, before the rest of the market.

At one point, the incoming results caused their model to conclude that Donald Trump was far more likely to win than the public seemed to understand. Jane Street had correctly figured out the election before most of the world.

The problem was that predicting the election and predicting the market’s reaction to the election were two completely different things.

Before the election, stocks had generally fallen whenever Trump’s chances improved. Jane Street therefore assumed that a Trump victory would cause the market to collapse. The firm made an enormous bet against US stocks and also bet against foreign markets, especially Mexico.

At first, the trade looked incredible. Jane Street was up around $300 million.

Then the market reversed.

US stocks rallied, and what had been a $300 million profit became roughly a $300 million loss. According to Sam’s account, the position went from one of the best trades in the firm’s history to its worst.

That part was almost funny. They had accomplished the supposedly impossible part. They built a system that figured out who was going to become president before most of the country, and they still managed to lose $300 million because they were wrong about what stocks would do afterward.

The mistake was not really the election prediction. They correctly determined that Trump was going to win. Their mistake was assuming that a Trump victory automatically meant the US market would collapse.

What I found interesting was Jane Street’s reaction. There was no dramatic scene in which everyone started screaming or someone was immediately fired. The firm cared heavily about whether the traders had followed a reasonable process.

That philosophy makes sense. A good decision can lose money, and a terrible decision can sometimes make money. It is impossible to judge every decision only by its result.

Still, Jane Street did learn from the loss. The leadership basically decided that elections were outside the firm’s area of expertise and that it should not make that kind of political trade again.

Sam hated that conclusion. He believed they had discovered a great opportunity and had simply chosen the wrong trade.

That disagreement says a great deal about him. Jane Street looked at a $300 million loss and thought, “Maybe we should stay away from this.” Sam looked at the same loss and thought, “We almost nailed it.”

The chapter also discusses Sam becoming vegetarian and later vegan after a friend questioned whether eating animals was consistent with his utilitarian beliefs.

I was surprised that Sam had apparently never thought deeply about that question before. For someone who supposedly filtered every decision through utilitarianism, animal suffering seems like one of the first and most obvious issues that would arise.

The book also barely discusses any moral philosophy outside utilitarianism. Everyone around Sam seems to treat it as the one objectively intelligent way to think rather than one ethical theory among many. That felt strange, especially because these people were using it to justify decisions involving enormous amounts of money and power.

By the end of the chapter, Sam becomes interested in cryptocurrency. He believes the market is inefficient enough that he could make a billion dollars. One of his friends tells him that he will not.

Obviously, Sam was right about that part—at least temporarily.

I still disliked the way Lewis portrayed him. Sam continued to come across as an ultra-genius who wanted nothing for himself and planned to make money only so that he could save humanity and give everyone a puppy.

At times, the admiration became so extreme that I wondered whether Sam had somehow hired Michael Lewis as part of an elaborate public-relations campaign.

This was my first Michael Lewis book, so perhaps this is simply how he writes about his subjects. Either way, it was becoming difficult not to notice how badly he wanted the reader to be fascinated by Sam.

Chapter 5: “How to Think About Bob”

This chapter begins the transition from Sam leaving Jane Street to starting Alameda Research, before FTX existed.

We are introduced to Caroline Ellison. Like Sam, she grew up in an academic family. Both of her parents were economists at MIT.

Compared with almost everyone else in the book, Caroline initially seems relatively normal. She is highly intelligent and ambitious, but she also wants some version of an ordinary life as a woman in her twenties.

She dates a man at Jane Street who tells her that he will never love her, which is an unbelievable thing to tell someone you are dating. Then again, almost every personal interaction in this book is strange enough to belong in a psychology experiment.

Caroline does not believe she is especially good at her job at Jane Street, and she does not find the work fulfilling. After reconnecting with Sam, she becomes interested in the crypto-trading company he is building and eventually leaves Jane Street to join him.

Alameda Research was co-founded by Sam and Tara Mac Aulay, another member of the effective altruism community. Sam then hired intelligent people from his immediate circle, including Gary Wang and Nishad Singh, who was close friends with Sam’s younger brother.

The hiring process seemed to be something like this: find the smartest person you know, make sure that person is sympathetic to effective altruism, and then provide access to millions of dollars. Actual management experience was apparently optional.

The early management team quickly became frustrated with Sam. They thought he was disorganized, lazy about basic responsibilities, and terrible at communicating. He wanted control over all the important decisions but often could not be bothered to respond when anyone needed him.

When serious problems appeared, Sam behaved as though everyone else was overreacting.

The clearest example is the disappearance of roughly $4 million worth of Ripple. Everyone else was panicking and wanted to tell investors that the money might be gone. Sam remained extremely calm and argued that there was still a high probability that the money would eventually reappear.

In his mind, if there was an 80 percent chance that the money would return, Alameda could still act as though it possessed 80 percent of it.

I understand the mathematical idea. But there is a point at which “thinking probabilistically” starts to look like refusing to admit that you may have lost someone else’s money.

Expected-value language is not a magical spell that makes missing cryptocurrency reappear.

The title of the chapter comes from a thought experiment about a person named Bob. Suppose Bob attends a party with twenty other people and someone is murdered. You do not know who committed the murder. How should the small probability that Bob is the murderer affect how you view him?

Sam’s point is that uncertainty does not require a completely binary conclusion. You do not have to decide that Bob is definitely innocent or definitely guilty. You can assign probabilities.

That makes sense as a philosophical idea. It becomes more questionable when the same logic is used to describe millions of dollars that have disappeared from a company.

Eventually, a large portion of Alameda’s original management team left. Many believed that Sam was impossible to work with and that the company was being operated irresponsibly. They also wanted compensation because they believed they had wasted valuable time on a dysfunctional organization.

The missing Ripple eventually did reappear because of a technical transfer issue. Sam was right that the money was probably not permanently gone.

That does not necessarily mean his behavior was responsible. Sometimes a reckless person gets lucky. A book so obsessed with probability should understand that better than anyone.

This is where Sam’s eccentricities began to look much less charming. His disorganization was no longer merely a funny personality trait. It was affecting employees, investors, and millions of dollars.

Lewis still seemed fascinated by Sam’s probabilistic worldview. I was starting to wonder how many obvious warning signs someone has to ignore before “unconventional” becomes “dangerous.”

Chapter 6: “Artificial Love”

This chapter introduces the creation of FTX and adds even more strange characters to the story.

Lewis first gives some background on cryptocurrency and the early crypto community’s distrust of governments, banks, and traditional financial institutions. The original dream was to create a financial system in which people did not have to trust powerful intermediaries.

The irony is that crypto soon created a new group of powerful intermediaries who were less regulated, less transparent, and often far stranger than the bankers they were supposed to replace.

We meet Zane Tackett, an early crypto figure attracted to the industry’s anti-establishment culture. We are also introduced to Changpeng Zhao, better known as CZ, the CEO of Binance.

The book does not really claim that the United States had no crypto regulation at all. The more accurate point is that regulation was fragmented, confusing, and full of gaps. Companies could move between countries and legal structures while arguing over which rules applied to them.

As Alameda’s easiest trading opportunities began disappearing, Sam decided that instead of merely trading on crypto exchanges, he should create an exchange himself.

Sam and Gary Wang developed FTX as a cryptocurrency derivatives exchange. One of its selling points was an automated liquidation system designed to close losing positions before those positions created losses for everyone else on the platform.

They initially pitched the idea to CZ at Binance. He rejected it, so Sam decided to build FTX himself.

We are also introduced to Ryan Salame, a former Ernst & Young tax accountant who somehow ends up joining the company. Sam hires him without having a particularly clear job in mind, which seems to have been the standard FTX hiring strategy.

Then there is Ramnik Arora. He had studied computer science at Stanford and worked at both Goldman Sachs and Facebook. He joins FTX with an impressive résumé but ends up serving as a general fixer for whatever random problem appears next.

There is something genuinely funny about a highly accomplished former Silicon Valley employee joining a multibillion-dollar crypto company and becoming the corporate version of the person everyone calls when the sink breaks.

Gary Wang, meanwhile, is portrayed like Superman. Sam comes up with an idea, and Gary somehow turns it into working software. Lewis describes him as someone capable of building almost anything while barely speaking to anyone around him.

Once again, everyone in Sam’s orbit is either a genius, unbelievably strange, or usually both.

The fundraising process is also interesting. Many investors initially express enthusiasm and then back away. Eventually, however, FTX begins raising enormous amounts of money at increasingly absurd valuations.

Once the momentum starts, investors seem terrified of being the only people who failed to recognize the next great financial empire.

What I still do not understand is why more people did not seriously question the relationship between Alameda and FTX.

The two companies were supposedly separate, but they were both controlled by Sam, worked closely together, and had extremely unclear boundaries. Alameda traded heavily on FTX, helped provide liquidity, and had access and influence that ordinary customers did not.

Even without knowing what would happen later, that was an enormous conflict of interest. The company operating the casino and one of the casino’s biggest gamblers were controlled by the same person.

Apparently, everyone decided this was acceptable because the person in charge wore cargo shorts and talked about charity.

Sam’s messages with Caroline are also painfully strange. They discuss their romantic relationship through written lists that sound more like corporate risk assessments than messages between two people who supposedly care about each other.

In one of them, Sam writes that, in many ways, he does not have a soul. He questions whether his empathy and emotional reactions are real and wonders whether he merely performs the responses that other people expect from him.

I am not sure whether this is supposed to sound profound, self-aware, disturbing, or simply eccentric.

Mostly, it is just weird.

Where I Stopped

That was as far as I got because I ended up losing the book.

I would bring it to work and read it during lunch, and one day I realized that I had no idea where it was. Maybe I left it somewhere in the office. Maybe someone picked it up. Maybe it is still sitting underneath a pile of papers.

Or maybe it was a sign.

Considering how irritated I was becoming with Michael Lewis’s portrayal of Sam Bankman-Fried, perhaps the universe decided that I had read enough.
