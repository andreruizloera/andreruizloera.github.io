# Personal Website — Positioning

> What this site is for, who it's for, and how to tell whether it's working. This is a personal site, not a product — so the lens is reputation and intellectual record, not revenue.

---

## Purpose & Goals

A single, owned home on the internet for Andre Ruiz Loera — a place that outlasts any platform feed and represents the work, the reading, and the thinking in one calm, fast, ad-free space.

Concrete goals:

- **Be the canonical link.** The one URL to put in a résumé, an email signature, a DM, or an application — and have it land well.
- **Show, don't claim.** Demonstrate mathematical and economic depth through annotations, reading lists, and writing rather than asserting it.
- **Build a durable record.** A growing archive of notes and essays that compounds over years, owned outright, not rented from a social platform.
- **Stay effortless to maintain.** Low enough friction (copy a template, edit text, push) that it survives busy semesters.

---

## Target Audience

In rough priority order:

- **Recruiters and admissions / hiring committees.** Quant finance, research, and academic readers who arrive from a résumé link and decide quickly whether this person is serious. They want signal fast: what they study, how they think, what they read.
- **Potential collaborators and peers.** Researchers, classmates, and online acquaintances who want to gauge shared interests (math, markets, philosophy) and reach out.
- **Curious readers.** People who found a blog post or reading list, liked it, and clicked through. The audience that turns a one-off visit into a returning reader.
- **Future self.** The annotations and lists are also a personal knowledge base — notes worth keeping for their own sake.

---

## Voice & Content Strategy

**Voice:** plain, precise, and unhurried. The existing post already sets it — explain real things clearly, skip the hype, let substance carry the page. Serious without being stiff; confident without performing.

**Per-section strategy:**

- **About** — short and human: who, where (Math & Economics, UIUC), what the visitor will find, and how to reach out. The first thing recruiters read; keep it sharp.
- **Blog** — fewer, better essays on mathematics, markets, and craft. Each post earns its place; quality over cadence. Bespoke visual touches are fine when they serve the piece.
- **Annotations** — the intellectual core. Structured reading notes (Overview, Key Ideas, Notes, Passages, Takeaways), with real math rendered properly. This is the strongest differentiator: it shows *how* he reads, not just *what*.
- **Reading Lists** — curated taste and ambition across twelve domains. Already substantial; the move is to link items to annotations as they're written, turning lists into a navigable map of the work.
- **Research / Press** — credibility surfaces; fill them as real items exist rather than padding.

**Cross-cutting:** no emojis, no tracking, accessible and fast — the medium reinforces the message that this is careful, owned work.

## What "Good" Looks Like (Success Signals)

This is a personal site, so success is qualitative and reputational, not metric-driven. Signals that it's working:

- A recruiter or professor cites something specific from the site ("I saw your notes on …") — proof it was actually read, not just skimmed.
- The URL becomes the default thing Andre shares, because it represents him better than any single profile.
- Reading-list and annotation pages get linked or bookmarked by others with overlapping interests.
- Inbound contact (collaboration, opportunities) traceable to the site.
- The archive keeps growing — the strongest internal signal is simply that new notes and posts keep landing.
- It loads instantly, reads cleanly on phone and laptop, and looks right in light and dark — the baseline that makes everything above possible.

Explicit non-goals: traffic numbers, SEO ranking races, follower counts, virality, or monetization. The site optimizes for the right reader, not the most readers.

## Inspiration / Comparables

Personal academic and digital-garden sites in a similar spirit — substance-first, low-chrome, owned:

- **Evan Chen** (evanchen.cc) — a mathematician's site dense with notes, problems, and LaTeX; directly relevant (his texts already appear in the annotations).
- **Gwern Branwen** (gwern.net) — the maximalist long-form research archive; a north star for "writing that compounds," even if far heavier than this site needs to be.
- **Academic homepages** in the Terence Tao / personal-faculty-page tradition — plain, fast, content-forward, no framework theater.
- **Digital gardens** (Andy Matuschak, Maggie Appleton) — the philosophy of public, evolving notes; the annotations section is a lightweight version of this.
- **Minimalist developer/student portfolios** — single stylesheet, sidebar nav, light/dark toggle; the visual register this site already lives in.

The shared thread: the site is a *record and a workshop*, not a brochure. It earns attention by being genuinely useful or interesting to read.

---

## Caveats

- **Content is early.** The structure and design are complete; substance is the bottleneck. Placeholder bio and empty research/press sections undercut the impression until filled — Phase 1–2 of the roadmap matters most.
- **Curation is a promise.** Long reading lists imply taste and follow-through; linking them to real annotations is what converts implication into evidence.
- **Personal, not professional product.** No uptime SLA, no growth targets. Judge it as a reputation and thinking surface, not a startup.
- **Discoverability is intentionally modest.** Until basic SEO/social-card work lands, the site is best reached by direct link — which is fine, because the highest-value visitors arrive that way.
